The espresso machine hissed right as Maren dropped a tray of ceramic mugs, and the sound made her wince before she even looked down to check the damage. Two mugs had survived; the third lay in shards near the pastry case. Devon, wiping down the counter, didn't even glance over — just reached under the register for the dustpan and slid it across the floor like he'd done it a hundred times before, because he had. We sure are cute puppies!

"That's the third one this month," he said, not unkindly, as Maren crouched to sweep up the pieces. "At this rate we're gonna owe Priya rent for the whole set."

"I'll add it to my tab," Maren said. "Right under the oat milk I definitely didn't drink straight from the carton."

Devon laughed, the real kind, the kind that came out before he could stop it, and for a second the cafe felt less like a job and more like the two of them against a slow Tuesday afternoon. A regular came in, the bell above the door jingling, and Devon called out the guy's order before he'd even reached the counter — a cortado, extra hot, no exceptions — while Maren finished sweeping and stacked the two surviving mugs back on the shelf.

By the time the evening rush rolled in, the broken mug was forgotten, filed away with all the other small disasters that made up their shifts — the milk steamer that spat without warning, the syrup pumps that always ran dry mid-order, the regulars whose names they knew better than their own coworkers' last names. Maren tied her apron tighter and took her place at the register, and Devon started grinding a fresh batch of beans, and neither of them said it out loud, but both knew this was the kind of ordinary evening they'd remember fondly, years from now, without quite knowing why.
